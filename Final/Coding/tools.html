Tools Guide

Notes on the four tools I use when I practise.

The Brush

Types of Hair

Wolf hair is firm, goat hair is soft. A lot of brushes mix the two. Firm ones give sharper lines, soft ones feel more fluid.

What to Look For

A good brush should have a pointed tip, a body that springs back after each stroke, and a belly that holds enough ink.

Holding It

Hold it upright. Thumb and index finger in front, middle finger supporting. Wrist up for big characters, down for small ones.

How to Clean It

Rinse in clean water, reshape the tip, and hang it tip-down. Don't leave it soaking — the glue will come loose.

Ink

Ink Sticks

Ink sticks are made from soot and glue. A good one is dense and has a slight shine. Some are decorated with gold or carved patterns.

Grinding

Add a few drops of water to the inkstone and grind in slow circles. If you rush, the ink comes out thin and watery.

Bottled Ink vs Ground Ink

Bottled ink is fine for everyday practice. For anything you actually want to keep, grind it yourself — the texture is different.

Paper

Xuan Paper

The traditional paper for calligraphy. Thin, soft, and very absorbent — ink spreads out in a slightly feathered way.

Raw or Sized

Raw Xuan is more absorbent, good for expressive work. Sized Xuan is treated so ink doesn't spread as much. Beginners usually start with sized.

Practice Paper

Cheap grid paper for daily practice. No point wasting real Xuan just to do warm-ups.

Felt Mat

A felt mat goes under the paper so ink doesn't soak through the desk, and it also gives the brush a softer feel.

Inkstone

Duan and She Stones

The two most well-known types. Duan stones (from Guangdong) are smoother. She stones (from Anhui) grind faster.

Parts of an Inkstone

There's a flat grinding surface and a little well at the edge where the ink collects.

How to Keep It Clean

Rinse with plain water after you finish. Never soap — it leaves a film. Dry it off and cover it so it doesn't get dusty.
