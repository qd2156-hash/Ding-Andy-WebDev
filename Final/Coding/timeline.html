A Short History

How Chinese calligraphy changed over time.

c. 1200 BC

Oracle Bone Script

The earliest Chinese writing we know of. Carved onto bones and shells and used for divination.

Zhou Dynasty

Bronze Inscriptions

Writing cast into bronze vessels. Shapes became more rounded and started to look like what we now call Seal Script.

221 BC

Seal Script is Standardised

The Qin emperor unified the writing system. Small Seal Script became the official form across the empire.

Han Dynasty

Clerical Script

Officials needed to write faster, so strokes got flatter and ended in a small sweeping tail. This is where calligraphy starts to look closer to modern writing.

Jin Dynasty

Running, Cursive, Regular

The rest of the main styles take shape. Wang Xizhi writes the Orchid Pavilion Preface during this period.

Tang Dynasty

The Golden Age

Probably the most famous period for calligraphy. Yan Zhenqing, Liu Gongquan, Huai Su — all Tang.

Song Dynasty

More Personal Style

Writers like Su Shi start caring more about expressing themselves than following strict rules.

Today

Still Alive

Calligraphy is still taught in schools and practised at home. I've been doing it since I was three.
